around image generators

i had a recent twitter interaction about the art to be found around image generators (if there is such a thing!) that made me a bit sad, but also did start this post bubbling around.

ethics?

to get it out there up front: i think image generators like dall-e and all prominent cases of machine learning are ethically bankrupt. they're trained on plundered (nonconsensual, uncredited) data, the act of which demands unsustainable amounts of energy, they transfer power/wealth to capital through the disempowerment & disinheritance & dissolution of (certain domains of) labor.

automation under capitalism is extraction. machine learning's draw, the reason capital invests in it, is its capacity to capitalize previously unreachable domains, not the novelty of its products.

& a proviso

a prerequisite for employing automation as a tool in service of the people is bounding its use to exclusively revolutionary contexts. there is no revolutionary use of dall-e or any similarly centrally-owned-and-operated image generator. they enrich capital at labor's expense.

& a possibility

in the case of image generators: maybe building one that is low-energy, sustainably-powered, collectively-owned, &c, and using it to produce work that exists outside of and actively seeks to replace its analogue within the capitalist economy. or, not building or using one at all.

or: i don't know, maybe nothing is enough.

art

however, once an image generator like dall-e exists, i don't think this means we can't use it to make art. it doesn't mean that it is aeshetically barren.

i operate with an understanding of art as labor in exclusive service of a personal subjectivity. an understanding i found earlier this year through hours spent entranced, placing & scaling & arranging a countable (but countable with difficulty) number of sphere primitives. it's one that i've found a lot of use in since.

the words: "personal" here demands person(s) and "subjectivity" suggests an invidualized, unhomogenized perspective as the result of a set of individual experiences. something unavailable to an entity like a corporation, or a tool like an image generator. unavailable, also, to a tool like a paintbrush.

(there's a lot of good stuff out there on art and aesthetics and i'm just going to ignore it (i should read more!))

less/full

a few weeks ago, i had a conversation with mut about the art/less/full/ness around dall-e-likes.

i was in the camp of their artlessness on account of my emotional response to their unethics and what i perceived to be a fundamental lack of both labor and personal subjectivity in their image production. i was too focused on their products, their artifacts, i think.

making sense of even the possibility of their artfulness is tricky since, per above, their concrete use is necessarily captitalized, is not transgressive, and is broadly uninteresting. submitting a query to dall-e and viewing its output is an artless experience: it is devoid of labor and subjectivity, as far as i'm concerned.

ness

the idea that revealed their possible art to me was mapping prompt & product to concept & execution, to artwork & artifact, to imagination & actualization. i'm a bit disappointed in myself for not having mapped this more quickly, as the imagination & execution relation is one my jams. still a bit too hard-headed at times.

given a sequence of queries, imagining the sequence of imaginary images an imaginary image generator produces in response is a rich aesthetic domain for the creation of new, previously impossible artworks!

possibly

previously impossible because, without dall-e (pain), we wouldn't have the culturally-shared understanding of the correspondence between a query and an image generator's graphical products. the one that we now do, the one that emerged in a matter of day(s). but now that we've materialized that understanding, we can dispose of dall-e, unburden our eyes of sight, descend into our collective imagination.

obviosuly, that doesn't mean we couldn't imagine images from text. but it does mean we now share a new context for doing so, one we can't escape from. that we may as well make ours, to whatever extent that is possible, rather than or openai's or google's or whatever else's.

relatedly

this sort of art is evocative of some of my favorites (magnifying the aformentioned disappointment): a fluxus score, the instructions for a sol lewitt wall drawing, a room full of dirt, a poem.

generally

i think a useful distinction to hold onto is between artwork & artifact. the artifact, in this case, being image. an artifact is not the artwork, it's a byproduct. i sense the artwork as the artist(s) personal subjectivity, a subjectivty that expresses itself through an artifact(s).

there may be, then, artworks to be made even from ai-generated artifacts, if only we could overcome ai's other ethical shortcomings.

an imagination

is a beautiful place, the site of art experience, an instancing of an aesthetics. paintbrushes are not interesting, paintings are not interesting. but what the paint does to my body. that is.
